# Photography

Photography does one job above all others: it makes the kinship with the Devon coast visible without ever claiming the coast is the supply chain. The product belongs on the rocks at Welcombe by sensibility and origin, not because the bottle was filled at the tideline. Honest and unfussed, never studio-slick.

The hero, across every setting, is the product in its aluminium bottle, often paired with the open tin. Shoot the packaging honestly: brushed aluminium, bamboo pump, the white label readable where it falls naturally in frame. No props that imply luxury or clinical sterility. The natural world and the working bench are the only set dressing the brand needs.

## Three settings, in order of priority

### 1. The coast (signature, should dominate)

The north Devon coast: wet rock, rock pools, the line where shore meets sea. Seaweed is welcome and on-message, because the algal kinship is part of the story, as long as the surrounding copy never tips it into a harvesting claim. The image can show the seaweed we know from our coast. The words still have to stay honest about where the material actually comes from.

### 2. The lab (process)

Where the products are made, by hand, in small batches: measuring, weighing, filling, capping. Clean, light backdrop, white or stainless, evenly lit. One discipline governs this setting absolutely: these images show craft, not research. Never style them to imply experimentation, clinical testing, or scientific study. No white-coat-and-clipboard shots, no microscopes or pipettes framed as instruments of discovery, no charts or readouts behind. Show the making. Never imply the studying.

### 3. Lifestyle (use, sparingly)

A plain, light domestic setting, a bathroom shelf or windowsill, and the product in use on skin: a pump dispensed into a hand, cream applied, texture on the fingertips. The human, relatable register that social and post-purchase email call for. On-skin imagery carries one caution from the claims discipline: show the experience, not a result. No before-and-after, nothing that implies a skin condition has changed. Keep skin real and unretouched. Use this setting sparingly; the coast should lead.

## Composition

Shallow depth of field, product sharp, setting soft behind it. Let the product sit lower or off-centre in a wide natural frame rather than filling it. Shoot at product height, not down at it. Leave generous negative space; it suits the brand's restraint and gives web and social layouts room to carry text.

## Light

Natural light wherever possible. Two moods both belong to the brand: low warm sun, early or late, for the human register used in post-purchase and social; cooler, soft, overcast light for the sober, precise register. In the lab, controlled artificial light is fine but keep it soft, even, and daylight-balanced so the bench reads as a real, light-filled workspace, not a clinical interior. Avoid hard flash and harsh studio lighting that flattens the texture of rock, metal, and water.

## Editing

Keep it true to what the camera saw. Gentle, natural grading that holds accurate colour, especially on the aluminium, which should read as warm-neutral brushed metal and never be pushed cold or oversaturated. The label colours must stay faithful; a skin-type colour drifting in a photo undoes the precision of the palette. No heavy filters, no fashionable colour casts, no skin-smoothing or retouching. The quiet chemist does not fake things, and neither does the photography.
